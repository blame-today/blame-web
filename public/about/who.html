what "one device" means

the rule is: one physical iphone can submit one vote per UTC day. not one apple id. not one app install. not one signed-in account. one physical device, identified by its secure enclave — the dedicated crypto chip apple ships in every iphone since the 5s.

i could have built this with email signups + per-account dedup. that would have given me "one account per day" — easier to defeat (signups are free), more surveillance (i'd have your email), more moderation surface (account recovery, ban appeals, the works). device-bound attestation gets a stronger constraint without collecting any identifying information.

UUID + App Attest, stacked

"one device" needs two pieces stacked together, because each piece alone is insufficient:

1 · UUID — a hashed device identifier

a random identifier generated on first launch and stashed in the ios keychain. raw uuid never leaves your phone. only sha256(uuid) ever crosses the wire — irreversible, cardinality-preserving. the worker uses this hash to dedupe votes per (device, day) without knowing what device that is.

if a UUID alone was the identifier, anyone with a vote-spam script could mint UUIDs all day and pump the leaderboard. UUIDs aren't proof of anything — they're just an arbitrary string.

2 · App Attest — apple's hardware proof

apple's app attest gives us a hardware-bound proof of "this is a real iphone running the real blame.app." the app attest keypair lives in the secure enclave — a dedicated crypto chip on the iphone, physically isolated from the main CPU. the private key cannot be exported, even from a jailbroken device.

every vote ships with an assertion signed by that enclave key over the request body. our worker validates the assertion against apple's root certificate before accepting the vote. when the validation fails (no assertion, replayed assertion, wrong device class), the vote is rejected at the boundary.

the two pieces together give us: "a vote from a uuid we recognize, attested by a real iphone we don't otherwise identify." anonymity from the worker's perspective, but device-bound enough to make per-day dedup meaningful.

scenarios people ask about — as far as i know

"could someone read my keychain and vote as me?"

on a non-jailbroken iphone: no. ios sandboxes keychain access per app. no other app can read blame.today's keychain entry. period.

on a jailbroken iphone with a keychain dumper: they can read your uuid. but the uuid alone doesn't authenticate anything — it just identifies. submitting a vote also requires a fresh assertion signed by your secure enclave's app attest key, and that key can't be exported even from a jailbroken device. worker rejects 401 in production without a valid assertion.

"could someone with my phone vote as me?"

yes — if they have your unlocked iphone in their hands, they can open the app and tap vote. that's the same threat model as your phone being stolen and used for any other app. outside our concern.

"if i uninstall and reinstall, can i vote again that day?"

on ios 10.3+, deleting an app also wipes its keychain. so yes — a fresh install gives you a fresh uuid + fresh app attest key, and to our worker that looks like a new device. you have to do the full delete-the-app-and-wait dance every time, and lose all your prior local state, just to add one more vote.

icloud keychain changes this. if you're signed into icloud and icloud keychain is enabled, your uuid syncs across devices + survives reinstall on the same apple id. for most people: reinstalling is NOT a fresh identity.

"if i factory-reset my phone, can i vote again?"

yes, same answer. a factory reset gives you a fresh keychain + fresh attest key. the cost is wiping all the data on your phone, which is a steep price for a fun app's leaderboard.

"could a determined attacker scale this with many real iphones?"

yes. nothing we do prevents someone with N iphones from casting N votes per day. the limit is buying iphones and getting through apple's attestation flow once each. that's a steep cost-per-vote, and that's the property we're buying with app attest — not "no one can ever cheat" but "cheating costs real money proportional to scale."

abuse vectors i can think of

laid out so people don't have to ask: yes, i thought about these, and i picked the trade-offs deliberately.

  • reinstall to vote again. partially mitigated by icloud keychain (your keypair survives reinstall if signed into icloud). without that, yes, reinstall = new identity. is that realistic for a daily blame board? probably not. is it visible? yes — the pattern "this device just appeared, voted for X, never seen before" is on the public ledger.
  • own multiple devices, vote multiple times. yes. one person with N iphones gets N votes. this is called "one vote per device per day," not "one vote per person per day." i'm not interested in collecting biometrics or government id to enforce the latter.
  • sim farm / phone farm. someone with hundreds of devices could amplify a target. yields hundreds of pubkeys voting in lockstep — extremely visible on the ledger, costs the attacker real money, fails any smell test on inspection.
  • icloud account switching. theoretically gives a new keychain entry. practical effort high, payoff low.

my opinion on the trade

"one vote per device per day" is a good-enough constraint to make the board interesting without imposing the surveillance cost of "one vote per person ever." attackers who try to game it are visible on the public ledger — auditors can detect unnatural patterns and call them out. i don't try to prevent abuse cryptographically; i surface it socially. that's the trade.

there's also a hedge most people miss: the seeded counts from the news zeitgeist (see how → seeding) act as a baseline of "what's actually being talked about right now." if 1000 sock-puppet votes pile onto some obscure target that's nowhere in today's news, the discrepancy is visible by contrast. the seeding isn't just for the empty-leaderboard problem — it's also a real-time sanity check on the user-vote distribution.

we're not inventing crypto.
we're stacking what apple gives us for free
on top of a public nostr ledger.

our claim is "one device, one vote per day," not "no one can ever cheat." if someone shows us how to cheat we'll patch it or tell you why we can't. we leave further improvements as an exercise for the reader — this is already overkill for a fun app whose worst-case failure mode is a slightly-skewed leaderboard.

privacy footnote

the uuid hash is the only identity-shaped thing that ever reaches the worker, and it's a sha256 — irreversible. the public nostr ledger sees even less: votes are signed with a per-device nostr keypair that has no link to your apple id, your phone number, or anything else about you. the only way to link a nostr pubkey to you is if you publish that link yourself somewhere else.
