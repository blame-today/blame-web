the platforms we picked, and why

every architectural choice has alternatives. the ones blame.today runs on weren't obvious. here's the reasoning behind each.

ledger: nostr (vs blockchain, AT protocol, ActivityPub)

what we needed: a way to publish signed events to multiple independent public servers, no fees, no governance, no tokens. just "post a message; anyone can verify it came from this keypair."

what we considered:

  • bitcoin / OP_RETURN — paid per transaction (~$1+), slow (10-minute blocks), tiny payload (80 bytes). built for money, not messages.
  • ethereum log events — paid per transaction (gas fees, often $5+), faster, bigger payload. one vote should not cost $5.
  • AT protocol (the protocol behind bluesky) — more complex (DIDs, personal data servers, full social graph). designed to power an entire social network. overkill for "signed events on a board." worth noting because people often ask: nostr and bluesky are not related — they're parallel open protocols with similar decentralization goals but completely different architectures.
  • ActivityPub (mastodon's protocol) — server-based identities; you need to trust the server hosting your account. doesn't have nostr's "your keypair is your identity" property.
  • roll our own protocol — we'd have to run the relays. defeats the entire "ledger we don't run" point.

why nostr fits:

  • cost: free. public relays run by volunteers and organizations (damus, primal, snort, nostr.band, etc.)
  • speed: real-time. websocket pub/sub. milliseconds, not minutes.
  • simplicity: events are {kind, content, tags, pubkey, signature}. that's the entire spec.
  • no tokens, no fees, no governance. nothing to vote on, no economic complexity.
  • identity = keypair. same property as bitcoin/PGP/signal, without monetary stakes.
  • multi-relay redundancy. publish to N independent relays, any one can serve the event.

nostr is the minimum viable protocol for what we need: signed events, public, redundant, free. anything more would be carrying weight we don't use.

compute + storage: cloudflare workers + D1

what we needed: edge compute that runs our worker globally with near-zero cold start, integrated with cheap storage, free tier generous enough to launch on $0.

what we considered:

  • AWS Lambda + DynamoDB / RDS — most powerful, also most operational complexity, slower cold start (100ms+), costs add up faster outside generous-but-finite free tier.
  • vercel / netlify functions — optimized for next.js / web stacks. less ideal for arbitrary worker code + integrated SQL storage.
  • fly.io / render / railway — containers (slower cold start), more setup overhead, different operational model.
  • GCP cloud run + cloud sql — fine but more provider lock-in for less generous free tier.

why cloudflare workers fits:

  • ~0ms cold start (V8 isolates, not VMs)
  • free tier: 100k requests/day on workers, 5M reads/day on D1
  • global edge: 250+ datacenters — the worker runs close to every user
  • DDoS absorption included free — CF anycast handles volumetric attacks
  • workers AI free tier for LLM inference (10k neurons/day; we currently use ~7k)
  • one-provider auth — single config, single bill (or no bill, in our case)

the whole stack — worker + D1 + Workers AI + Pages — is one provider's free tier. that's the architectural choice that makes "nearly-free launch" possible. not "scaled aggressively" — just "doesn't cost anything yet."

inference: cloudflare workers AI (initially)

what we needed: LLM inference for entity extraction (news → blameable target) on a free-ish budget, running inside the same worker code path.

what we considered:

  • OpenAI / anthropic claude API — best models, pay-per-token. great for serious work, doesn't fit the $0/mo goal at any meaningful seeding volume.
  • local on-device LLMs (iOS CoreML) — too compute-heavy for daily seeding; doesn't fit the cron-driven worker pattern.
  • hugging face inference API — free tier exists but rate-limited.
  • together / replicate / anyscale — multi-provider routing under one API. useful future option (see council-of-models below) but not yet.

why workers AI fits today:

  • free tier: 10k neurons/day — current seeding burns ~7k, leaves room for council expansion
  • models available: llama-3.1-8b, mistral-7b, gemma-7b, qwen-2.5 — good enough for entity extraction
  • same provider as the worker — single auth, single binding, zero network overhead between worker and inference

at scale we'll fan out across multiple providers' free tiers via the council pattern (openrouter as aggregator), but cloudflare's free tier alone gets us through launch and a meaningful amount of growth.

the principle running through all three: pick the simplest open thing that exactly fits the need. nostr over blockchain because blockchain is paid + slow; cloudflare over AWS because cloudflare is faster + free at our scale; workers AI over premium APIs because we don't need premium for entity extraction. nothing here is permanent — the architecture is designed to spread across providers as the system grows, because that's the actual long-term resilience story. but the starting platforms were chosen by elimination, not by ideology.

show your work

the pitch is "you don't have to trust me." that means i owe the actual mechanisms, not just the architecture diagrams. three places people ask about most: how do you tell devices apart, what literally ends up on the public ledger, and how do you turn today's news into seed numbers. here's all three — with the abuse vectors i can think of and what i actually think about each.

your "device id" — what it really is

when the iOS app first launches, it generates a per-device nostr keypair locally (BIP-340 schnorr, stored in the system keychain). your public key is the device id we track. the worker only stores SHA-256(public-key) for the one-vote-per-device-per-day uniqueness check — not the key itself, not your name, not your Apple ID, not your IP.

on this marketing site, "you →" in the footer is a similar random identifier that lives in your browser's localStorage. it's nostr-pubkey-shaped (32 hex bytes) but it's not yet a real signing keypair — no signing happens from the web today. it's a slot.

it's not a fingerprint. we don't derive it from your device's characteristics (canvas hash, GPU, font list, audio stack, screen geometry). it's a random keypair generated locally on first launch. if you want a new id, delete the app and reinstall (with iCloud Keychain disabled), or clear your browser's localStorage for blame.today. you control it. we don't have a way to identify you across resets.

abuse vectors i can think of:

  • reinstall to vote again. partially mitigated by iCloud Keychain (your keypair survives reinstall if signed into iCloud). without that, yes, reinstall = new identity. is that realistic for a daily blame board? probably not. is it visible? yes — the pattern "this device just appeared, voted for X, never seen before" is on the public ledger.
  • own multiple devices, vote multiple times. yes. one person with N iPhones gets N votes. this is called "one vote per device per day," not "one vote per person per day." i'm not interested in collecting biometrics or government ID to enforce the latter.
  • sim farm / phone farm. someone with hundreds of devices could amplify a target. yields hundreds of pubkeys voting in lockstep — extremely visible on the ledger, costs the attacker real money, fails any smell test on inspection.
  • iCloud account switching. theoretically gives a new keychain entry. practical effort high, payoff low.

my opinion: "one vote per device per day" is a good-enough constraint to make the board interesting without imposing the surveillance cost of "one vote per person ever." attackers who try to game it are visible on the public ledger — auditors can detect unnatural patterns and call them out. i don't try to prevent abuse cryptographically; i surface it socially. that's the trade.

there's also a hedge most people miss: the seeded counts from the news zeitgeist (next card) act as a baseline of "what's actually being talked about right now." if 1000 sock-puppet votes pile onto some obscure target that's nowhere in today's news, the discrepancy is visible by contrast. the seeding isn't just for the empty-leaderboard problem — it's also a real-time sanity check on the user-vote distribution.

what actually goes on the public ledger

every vote becomes a nostr event with this content, literally: {"target":"<canonical-id>","label":"<display-label>","app":"blame","v":1} — plus the signer's pubkey, the timestamp, the signature, and a "d" tag identifying the day. that's it. seven relays we don't own each get a copy. it's plaintext, it's permanent, and we can't unpublish from a public protocol.

which means: if you type private info into the target field and it slips past our filters, that private info goes on the public ledger in plaintext. no encryption, no hashing.

why not just hash the target? because the leaderboard depends on grouping ("how many people blamed 'mondays'?"), which needs the plaintext to do the count. if we hash, we'd have to keep the plaintext mapping ourselves — which makes us the trusted authority and breaks the cache-vs-record property. auditors recomputing from raw events need to see plaintext too, or they can't recompute. the architecture requires plaintext on the chain. the only viable defense is at the input boundary, before the vote is ever signed — which is a substantial topic of its own, treated separately in moderation, in retrospect ↓.

size discipline. there's also a daily signed seed manifest the worker publishes — one event per day containing the seeded-portion of the leaderboard so iOS clients can read directly from nostr if the worker is unreachable. that event is bounded:

  • ~14KB at 250 entries in current production (no headlines in the manifest; just target_id + label + trending_reason + seeded_count, in a compact positional format)
  • soft cap: 32KB. worker logs a warning if exceeded — most relays accept up to 64-128KB but a few have stricter limits
  • hard cap: 64KB. worker refuses to publish beyond this, with a clear error. forces us to fix the source (e.g. trim trending_reason strings) rather than ship a manifest the relays will silently reject

deliberately not gzipping content: nostr relays that support WebSocket permessage-deflate compress on the wire automatically; pre-gzipping doubles work + obscures the data when reading raw events. cheaper to keep the content honest and shape it small.

what an attacker can and can't do (the crypto part)

a careful reader will ask: if all your traffic to the nostr relays is public, can't someone watch the traffic and learn enough to forge votes? answer: no — but it's worth saying exactly why, since "all the data is public" sounds scarier than it is.

the entire protocol rests on public-key cryptography. the iOS app generates a BIP-340 schnorr keypair on first launch. the private key stays in the iOS keychain, encrypted, never leaves your device. the public key is part of every event you publish — anyone can see it. signing uses the private key; verification uses the public key.

the math property doing the work: given a public key and any number of signatures, you cannot derive the private key. not by inspecting traffic, not by computing, not by brute force. this is the same property that makes bitcoin, signal, PGP-signed git commits, and TLS work. publishing the public key compromises nothing — that's the entire point of asymmetric crypto. the secret isn't in transit. it's on the device.

what an attacker watching our traffic to relays can do:

  • replay a signed event they've already seen — nostr relays dedupe by event id. doesn't add a vote.
  • publish their own events with their own keypair — that's just normal voting. they get one vote per their key per day, same as everyone else. encouraged, even.
  • modify an event in transit — modification breaks the signature, relays reject the event.
  • derive a private key from observed signatures — 256-bit elliptic curve discrete log problem. computationally infeasible (heat-death-of-the-sun timescales with current hardware, and quantum-resistant variants exist if that ever changes).

what an attacker can do (and we can't fully prevent):

  • compromise a user's device and extract the keychain — any keypair-based system has this property. impact: forge that one user's vote. requires real attacker effort. bounded to that one device.
  • get a user to voluntarily share their private key — user's choice. bounded to that device.
  • run a "sim farm" of physical or virtual devices — each device gets its own keypair, votes once per day. costs the attacker real money. also visible on the public ledger as a cluster of fresh pubkeys voting in lockstep — exactly the pattern that the news-zeitgeist seeding acts as a structural counterweight against (see moderation, in retrospect ↓).

my opinion: the threat model here is the same as every keypair-based system you already trust. the secrets aren't in transit; they're on the user's device. publishing public keys is not a leak — it's the design. the most realistic attack against blame.today specifically is a coordinated sim farm, and the architecture surfaces that pattern publicly instead of hiding it. we can't stop someone with a warehouse of phones, but we can guarantee everyone else can see what they're doing.

seeding — how today's news becomes numbers

the daily board doesn't start empty. each morning at 6am UTC the worker pulls headlines from a fixed set of public sources, asks a small LLM to extract a blameable entity from each, and computes a seeded_count that estimates how much aggregate negative attention each entity is getting. seeded counts are clearly labeled separately from real_count in the API — they're never conflated with actual user votes.

the sources, with their attention weights per mention:

  • yahoo news RSS — 200,000 (high; direct mainstream publisher)
  • google news RSS — 50,000 (aggregator; many publishers deduped)
  • reddit /r/news, /r/worldnews — 30,000 each
  • reddit /r/politics — 25,000
  • reddit /r/technology — 20,000
  • wikipedia top pageviews (yesterday, en) — weight = actual pageview count, sqrt-compressed above 1M

the model and the formula:

  • LLM: @cf/meta/llama-3.1-8b-instruct on cloudflare Workers AI
  • per headline → { target, label, negativity } with negativity ∈ [0, 3]
  • aggregate per canonical id: attention = Σ source_weight, avg_negativity = Σ neg / mentions
  • seeded_count = round(0.001 × effective_attention × avg_negativity), where effective_attention is sqrt-compressed above 1M to keep one outlier from dominating
  • daily decay: existing trending × 0.85 each refresh, today's contributions added on top, anything below 5 pruned. ~30-day fade for a one-time mention.
  • leaderboard sort key = raw total (seeded + real). the number on the row IS the rank — no hidden weighting. tie-breakers: more real votes wins (user signal beats seed), then stable on target id. entities need ≥ 3 total votes to appear on the public board so solo write-ins don't pollute the list; the voter themselves always sees their own vote reflected via client-side rank synthesis. earlier versions used bayesian shrinkage to suppress fresh 1-vote spam, but the displayed-number-vs-sort-key divergence (row showing 741 ranking like 840) was worse than the noise it tried to fix — the ≥ 3 floor handles the spam case more transparently.

generic-noun filter at extraction. the LLM occasionally emits pronouns or ultra-generic single words as entities — man, they, people, it, everyone. these have no real referent; if accepted, the fuzzy-merge rule absorbs every adjacent phrase into them (e.g. "shell-shocked man" folding into "man" and "man" ranking instead of the real entity). a blocklist at the parser layer drops single-token extractions that match a small set of obvious pronouns + generic nouns. multi-word phrases bypass it — only the context-free single tokens are filtered. surfaced on the live system, fixed before it could distort a real day.

where this is heading: a council of models.

running everything through one llm has two problems beyond bias: every model is one company's opinion of "blameable entity," and any one free tier runs out of tokens fast. cloudflare workers ai is ~10k neurons/day; we currently use ~7k just for the daily refresh. that leaves no room for retries, no buffer for richer prompts, no headroom to scale headlines.

the plan is to fan the same headlines out to several small models in parallel — gemini-flash, llama-3.1-8b, mistral-small, qwen-2.5-7b, deepseek-chat — and take consensus on each entity. divergent picks get dropped or flagged as low-confidence. that does three things at once: hedges any single model's bias, produces safer cleaner word clouds when models agree, and spreads the load across multiple providers' free tiers. same "stack free tiers" pattern as the rest of the architecture, applied to inference.

llm-aggregator services make this nearly trivial to wire up — openrouter is the obvious one (one api, 100+ models, fan-out is a config change), with together, groq, anyscale all offering similar multi-model routing. some have free tiers; even paid is $0.10–$0.50 per million tokens for small models, which is rounding error in the running-costs ledger. the architectural property doesn't change — cache is opinion, protocol is record — but the cache gets smarter and more durable.

abuse vectors i can think of:

  • i could manipulate seeding to bias the board. technically yes. defense: the whole pipeline is deterministic from public inputs. you can run the same RSS feeds, the same model(s), the same code, and get the same numbers. when the main repo goes public near launch, the code is auditable. weight / source / model / formula changes are git-visible.
  • any single LLM has biases. yes, every model does. defense today: model id is public, you can run the same headlines through a different model and compare. defense in progress: the council pattern above turns this into a strength — divergence across models is the bias signal itself.
  • the news sources have biases. obviously. the weight calibration is open. you can disagree with the weights — they're literally numbers in a config. recompute with your weights and see how the board shifts.
  • i could quietly swap the formula. sure, but the deployed worker code is reproducible from the repo, and changes show up in git history. if the displayed seeded counts diverge from what the formula in the repo produces on the same inputs, something is wrong and it's detectable.

my opinion: seeded counts are an editorial choice presented as an editorial choice. they're labeled separately so a real vote count of 1 isn't conflated with a seeded count of 1,000. the iOS app's tap-row context view shows the actual headlines that drove an entity into the seeds — nothing is laundered. the seeds exist because an empty leaderboard is boring and because the zeitgeist baseline catches sock-puppet spam, not because i want to nudge anyone's votes.

attestation, where it sits

a sharper version of "show your work" is: what stops someone from just publishing fake votes? the honest answer is that blame.today today is transparent centralized aggregation, not trustless decentralized voting. those sound similar. they aren't. it's worth being explicit about the difference, because attestation is what turns the first into something defensible without pretending it's the second.

what's actually counting your vote, today

the leaderboard you see is produced by our cloudflare worker. iOS sends a signed vote → worker validates → worker writes to D1 → worker publishes a daily signed seed manifest to nostr. the manifest is signed by a published worker pubkey so anyone can verify "this is what the worker said today." the worker is the trust anchor; nostr is its public audit trail.

individual per-vote nostr events are also published (best-effort, from iOS, after the worker write succeeds). they're observable on the relays. but nothing aggregates them into a count. the worker's count is the count.

this is honest and it's enough. the property we promise is "you can verify the operator's math; the operator can't lie undetectably." not "there is no operator." those are different properties and we don't claim the stronger one.

the gap, and what closes it

because the worker is the count, the worker's input is what matters. today anyone can POST a vote to /vote with any well-formed payload — we have rate-limiting per IP, a council moderation gate, and one-vote-per-device-per-day uniqueness on the signing key. none of those stop a determined attacker who is willing to rotate identities and IPs.

the right defense is device attestation: bind each vote to a real install on a real device that the platform vendor will vouch for. then "one vote per device per day" gets backed by something that costs an attacker more than a fresh UUID.

per platform, what that actually looks like:

  • iOS — App Attest (planned, pre-launch): Apple-issued attestation tied to a specific App Store install on a specific device. iOS app generates an attestation key once, proves to the worker that it came from a real install, then signs each vote payload with an assertion the worker validates. forging at scale requires real iOS devices + real App Store accounts. cheap to add, hard to bypass.
  • Android — Play Integrity (future): Google's analogue. same shape: per-install attestation + per-request assertion. when an Android client ships, this is what backs it.
  • web — no equivalent. Turnstile and hCaptcha guard requests, not installs — they prove "a human did a thing right now," not "this browser session is a unique identity tied to a verifiable account." a future web client would either accept weaker guarantees (per-IP + per-key throttle, browser-fingerprint heuristics) or require a proof-of- work hashcash per vote. honest framing: web votes would carry a different reliability label than mobile votes.

what attestation doesn't fix: someone with N real iPhones and N App Store accounts can still cast N votes. that's the same as the sim-farm case in show your work: cluster of fresh pubkeys voting in lockstep, visible on the public ledger, costs the attacker real money. attestation raises the floor; it doesn't claim to be a ceiling.

the deeper architectural question

attestation at the worker hardens the worker's input. if we ever moved the model from "worker aggregates, signs the result, publishes to nostr" to "anyone aggregates from raw nostr events," attestation would have to move into the events themselves — each event carrying its attestation token, each aggregator validating. that's a heavier lift: every consumer becomes a verifier.

for now, the worker-as-aggregator model is the right shape for a one-person free-tier project. it gives us the property we actually promise (transparent operator, public audit trail) at the complexity we can sustain. the trustless-aggregation version is on the table architecturally — the events are already public — but it's not what we ship today, and the docs shouldn't pretend otherwise.

moderation, in retrospect

this section is about a problem that wasn't on my radar at the start. for any reader who thought about it earlier than i did: yeah, you saw this coming. i didn't, for a while. then i did. here's the arc — partly so i don't get to pretend it was always part of the plan.

the original vision: zero moderation

initially i was going to ship it open. one vote per device per day, anonymous, signed, public. who's it hurting? this is, in retrospect, the kind of sentence you only get to say before you actually try to launch the thing.

if every user was using it as intended — one blame per day on an abstraction or a public figure, no PII — there'd be no problem. the worst-case input ("john smith at 123 main street") would land in millionth place behind war, ai, the algorithm, mondays and the daily news villains. it'd never surface. the leaderboard would be exactly the data the app is supposed to surface.

but reality has a small fraction of users who'll type their ex's address, paste a phone number, or attempt to dox someone for fun. once that string is signed and published to seven nostr relays we don't run, it's there forever. we can't unpublish from a public protocol. that's the entire point of the architecture, and it cuts both ways.

first attempt: engineer the filter

the first answer wasn't simplify the input — it was build a smarter gate. two parts of the original plan evolved:

1. seeding became more important than i thought.

originally the news-seeding was just "fill an empty leaderboard so the first user has something to tap." in retrospect it's doing real structural work: the news signal is louder than any single user-typed target. a name nowhere in today's news and only voted on by a few users sinks to the bottom; a name everywhere in the news rises. seeded counts aren't decoration — they're the structural defense against vote-clustering on private targets.

this is also why i'm planning to extend the seed sources. today: news + wikipedia pageviews. next on the list: kalshi, polymarket and other prediction-market trending feeds — topics people are betting real money on are almost definitionally the hottest topics. then maybe reddit upvote velocity on a curated set of subs. each additional public source makes the zeitgeist signal louder relative to any single user-typed name.

2. moderation had to become automatic.

one person can't review every vote, and can't be awake every hour. moderation has to:

  • run in the worker before the vote is signed and published — once it's on the relays, no take-backs
  • be cheap enough to not burn the daily inference budget
  • be deterministic enough to be reproducible from the same code — same input, same decision
  • fail closed under uncertainty. when in doubt, reject. the cost of a false positive (we rejected a legit vote) is recoverable — the user retries. the cost of a false negative (we let PII through) is forever.

the first cut of this was a layered gate: regex blocks the obvious PII shapes, then a canonical pool of ~34k Wikipedia entities allows the easy publics, then a vulgarity check, then a Wikipedia round-trip for unknowns, and finally an LLM council voting on the gray-zone targets. for a while that seemed fine.

second attempt: simplify the input

a few weeks of building the layered gate later, we ran into two things the engineering couldn't paper over:

  • Apple's UGC rules (Guideline 1.2) require any app with user-generated content to provide a report mechanism with timely responses + the ability to block abusive users. responsibly implementing that is real ongoing work — not the right shape for a hobby project.
  • the moral problem of voting on private individuals. even with a perfect filter, the question "is it OK for an app whose product is anonymous public sentiment to accept any string a user types about any person?" answers itself eventually. the answer is no.

the cleanest response: don't accept user-generated labels into the public ledger at all. a vote on a pre-existing entity in a curated pool isn't user-generated content — same shape as a yelp star rating on a business or a reddit upvote on someone else's post. the user picks from a list; the count goes up; nothing they typed becomes part of the public record.

the freeform-text path stays in the app — you can still type whoever or whatever you want — but anything that isn't a pool entry routes to private vote: stays on your device, never reaches the public ledger. you get the cathartic tap; nobody else has to host the consequence.

so the gate stack ended up much simpler than the first cut: the pool replaces the council, and the council + Wikipedia round-trip become proposal paths for growing the pool under our control rather than auto-approval paths to public.

the gate — what reaches the public ledger

every vote passes through a small layered gate. the order matters — the fast paths short-circuit, the slow ones never approve.

at the input layer (on the user's phone):

  • 40-character cap on the text field. targets are short. typed input gets truncated past 40 chars before the request is even composed. matches the canonical pool's display-width cap.
  • autocomplete bias. the typing UX surfaces the canonical pool (~34k high-pageview Wikipedia entities) + today's trending. the path of least resistance leads to a known public figure — most users tap a suggestion and never freeform-type a name.

on the worker, in order:

  • obvious-PII regex → private. phone numbers (\d{3}[-.\s]?\d{3}[-.\s]?\d{4}), social-security patterns (\d{3}-\d{2}-\d{4}), email shapes (@…\.tld), URLs (https?://), street-address shapes (\d+ name (street|ave|rd|blvd|…)), 13–19 digit credit-card shapes. cheap, definitive — short-circuits before anything else.
  • in the canonical pool → public. 34,363 entities from the last decade's Wikipedia top-pageviews, bundled with the worker as a static dictionary. in-memory Map lookup, microseconds. anyone in this set is a publicly-discussed entity by construction. the pool itself is whole-label-filtered against LDNOOBW before bundling, so slur articles don't appear in autocomplete in the first place.
  • in today's trending → public. if the target's already in today's seeded leaderboard, it survived the seeding pipeline's vetting earlier this morning. one SQLite lookup, no further work.
  • cached prior pool-decision → public. targets we've previously promoted into the pool keep their decision.
  • anything else → private. the vote saves to the user's phone only — no row in the worker's database, no nostr publish, no leaderboard appearance. the 24h cooldown still applies, so private-routing isn't a retry loophole.

what we use Wikipedia + the LLM council for (under the new architecture):

  • not as public-approval paths. the previous version of this gate sent unknown targets to a Wikipedia lookup, then to a three-model LLM council, and approved anything either positively identified. that's gone — those routes used to let user-typed strings onto the public ledger, which is exactly the problem we're now avoiding.
  • as proposal paths for growing the pool. we can still use them offline to surface candidate additions — a target that gets typed enough times, has a strong Wikipedia presence, and the council confidently rules public, might be a pool-growth candidate. but the pool grows under our control, not on a vote.

what "private" means (the routing isn't a rejection):

  • local-only vote. the vote saves to your phone with the same 24h cooldown a public vote would have. you see a per-route explanation ("that looks like a phone number — saving locally" / "we don't have a public record for this — saving locally").
  • the architecture's permanence promise cuts both ways: once a vote is on the public ledger, it's there forever. private-routing is how we honor "type anyone, anything" without paying that cost on the long tail.

on the seeding side — how duplicate entities get folded:

  • LLM extractions of "musk" / "elon musk" / "Mr. Musk" / "Tesla CEO" all canonicalize to the same id via the worker's canonicalize() function (lowercase, NFKD-transliterate accents, replace non-alphanumeric with hyphen, expand known abbreviations).
  • cross-entity fuzzy merging: token-subset matching plus Levenshtein-≤2 typo tolerance. "trump" + "donald trump" fold; "iran" + "iraq" don't (close letters but meaningfully different). stopwords for the token-subset check come from scikit-learn's ENGLISH_STOP_WORDS (originally Glasgow IR Group) + a small domain-specific list of honorifics + political titles ("president", "senator", "ceo") that standard stopword lists don't include but get prepended to real names.
  • canonical pool wins ties on display-name. if either side of a fuzzy pair is a Wikipedia pool entry, that entry's casing ("Donald Trump") becomes the leaderboard label.
  • a consolidation pass runs each daily refresh — folds any duplicate trending rows that survived prior refreshes (e.g. legacy accumulations from before the current dedup logic landed), moves their daily_rollup + daily_context rows onto the canonical id.

what we don't do:

  • we don't identify users. uuid is hashed before storage; no name, no IP, no apple ID, no email is ever associated with a vote.
  • we don't moderate the leaderboard view based on who's looking. the gate runs at acceptance, not retrieval — once a vote passes, every viewer sees the same leaderboard.
  • we don't claim the filter is perfect. small LLMs miss edge cases. the local-only mode is the safety valve: a false-negative on the gate just keeps the vote off the public ledger, not off the user's device.

where the moderation lives (and doesn't)

important honest note: moderation lives at the acceptance boundary, before signing. it does NOT live at the retrieval boundary, after publication. a user running their own nostr client can technically publish any string they want to the relays — they don't need our worker. our worker is the cache; the relays are the record; the input filter is at the cache.

what that means for the leaderboard: our leaderboard reflects votes that passed our moderation. auditors recomputing from the raw relay feed will see everything published — including stuff our filter rejected if anyone bypassed our worker. that's a feature, not a bug: independent observers can see what we filtered out and disagree with us about it. our filter is auditable in both directions.

my opinion: i underestimated this at the start. "zero moderation, fully open" is a sentence you can say before you actually try to ship the thing. once you're staring at a real input box that a real user can type a real address into, the responsibility lands. and the first answer — engineer a smarter gate — got us most of the way but kept making us the arbiter of who counts as "public enough." the second answer is structurally simpler: don't accept user-generated labels onto the public ledger at all. the pool grows under our control; freeform stays on-device; the gate stays small and mechanical.

if you find a way to slip something past the gate that shouldn't be public: tell us. we'd rather hear it from a friendly tester than discover it after someone's home address is on seven relays for the next decade. the right channel is github.com/blame-today/blame/issues (public, no ETA on response) or hello@blame.today for anything private.

the flow, in retrospect

the user flow has the same shape as the architecture — i didn't design it on purpose, it found its current shape as i made decisions about what should be meaningful. three turns:

1 · originally: infinite-click ledger

first version was an open ledger. tap whatever, tap as often as you want, no limit, no friction. just contribute to a stream. sounds reasonable, until you think for two seconds: if every vote costs nothing, every vote means nothing. the leaderboard becomes "whoever has the most patience for tapping." that's not a signal, that's an endurance test.

2 · then: one vote per device per day

next version: one tap. one device. one day. that's it. now your vote is scarce — you only get one, you have to spend it on something, and once you do you're locked out until tomorrow. scarcity does the work that infinite-tap couldn't: scarcity makes a vote weigh something.

as a side effect, this also fixed the "endurance leaderboard" problem. the board now reflects what a representative slice of device-holders chose to spend their one daily vote on. that's a meaningfully different signal.

3 · then: gate the board behind voting

latest turn: you have to vote (or actively choose not to) to see the full ranking. on the home page, the leaderboard is hidden behind a tap. tap the gate, the board reveals.

this is a deliberate call-to-action. blame.today is participatory. if you scroll, scroll, scroll without engaging, the page is telling you something — there's a threshold to cross. you can cross it casually (the gate tap isn't a real vote, just a confirmation that you're here on purpose) or you can skip it entirely (the data's on the public ledger, here's how to recompute). both paths are explicit.

important honest note: even with no users, this was the idea. a call-to-action with no traffic to act on it is still a call-to-action — it just isn't seen by anyone yet. the gate is designed for the day people show up, and it's fine if that day never comes. the framing matters whether the audience does or doesn't.

+ the sampler: a teaser before the gate

the home page also shows three random board entries above the gate — picked deterministically from your visitor key + today's date. same picks for you on every visit today; different picks for everyone else; new picks tomorrow.

this isn't the ranking. it's a taste. the goal is to convey "this is alive and there's something for you here" without revealing the rank — and to use the visitor key meaningfully, not just decoratively. you have an identity at blame.today the moment you arrive; the sampler is the first thing that identity does for you.

seed is SHA256-shaped(visitor_key + ':' + today); pool is top 100 entries; pick is the first 3 of the seeded shuffle. all client-side javascript — the worker just returns the leaderboard, the deterministic personalization happens in your browser. no per-visitor tracking on the server side. anyone can recompute their own sample given just their visitor key + the day.
